Professional Presence

Professional presence is a dynamic blend of poise, self-confidence, control, and style that empowers people to command respect in any situation.

Executive presence centers on gravitas, poise under pressure, decisiveness, effective communication, and appearance.

1. Online

LinkedIn

  • Use an approachable headshot.
  • Indicate whether you are actively looking for employment.
  • Share interests and topics that may not be directly related to work.

Facebook

  • Use as a secondary professional presence.
  • Leverage your social network as a professional network when job searching.
  • Indicate employment history and show pride in your career.
  • Avoid posting anything you would not want an employer to see.

Email

  • Proofread messages carefully.
  • Remember emails are permanent and can be forwarded.
  • Be thoughtful about who you include on copy to avoid using it as a power move.

2. Phone

Details Matter

  • Evaluate responses and do not rush.
  • Monitor voice quality, including volume and clarity.
  • Be prepared with a list of key items, pen, paper, schedule, and contact information.

Good Manners

  • Avoid multitasking such as chewing, drinking, or clicking objects.
  • Use friendly phrases and courteous greetings.

Be Concise

  • Leave short, focused voicemails.
  • Summarize calls at the end to clarify next steps.

3. Body Language

  • Avoid using your phone in ways that cause slouching.
  • Maintain good posture.
  • Do not cross your arms.
  • Make appropriate eye contact.
  • Offer a firm handshake.

4. Clothing

  • Know and follow your company dress code; ask if unsure.
  • Avoid fussing with clothing or accessories to prevent appearing insecure.
  • Ensure proper fit of clothes.
  • Dress for the specific day and schedule, even on casual days.
  • Keep a spare set of appropriate clothing for unexpected situations.
  • Avoid clothing that exposes undergarments or too much skin.

5. Hygiene and Etiquette

  • Come to work clean and polished with neat hair and nails, and clean, ironed clothes.
  • Use minimal or no perfume/cologne so scent is not noticeable at a distance.
  • Keep a small toiletry kit for discreet use in the restroom, not at your desk.
  • Cover coughs and sneezes, use tissues or your sleeve, and wash hands properly.

6. Verbal Communication

Speak Up

  • Use clear, strong language and speak with energy.
  • Avoid rushing through your words.
  • Be friendly to everyone regardless of their role.
  • Use the other person’s name appropriately without overdoing it.

Maintain Composure

  • Stay calm when put on the spot.
  • Ask for clarification when needed.

Avoid Submissive Language

  • Avoid unnecessary apologies.
  • Minimize filler words such as “like,” “um,” and “uh.”
  • Avoid nervous laughter.

Know What You Don’t Know

  • Ask questions rather than pretending to understand.
  • Ask well-researched questions and explain what you know and where you are stuck.

Be Engaged

  • Listen actively and observe others’ work.
  • Show interest and recognize when colleagues exceed expectations.
  • Share ideas and contribute constructively.

7. Feedback

Ask for honest feedback from two or three people about how they perceive your professional presence across online, phone, and in-person interactions.

Skit Concept

Filmed in the style of an office mockumentary to illustrate professional presence.

The skit features four personalities and one neutral character: Nervous, Arrogant, Disorganized, Good Professional Presence, and Neutral as the meeting conductor.

Character Highlights

  • Nervous: Self-conscious, fidgeting, submissive language, fussing with clothing.
  • Arrogant: Encroaches on space, interrupts, phone on desk, condescending, flirts.
  • Good PP: Prepared, confident greeting, good posture, “Yes, and…” mindset.
  • Disorganized: Calls into meeting, rambles, noisy, unprepared.
  • Neutral: Facilitates the meeting and drives the discussion.

Scenario Structure

Possible Scenario 1

  • Five people: meeting lead, two general actors, one Good PP, one Bad PP.
  • Good PP arrives prepared, engaged, and professionally dressed.
  • Bad PP arrives late, distracted, underprepared, and less engaged.
  • The audience is asked to identify good and bad examples of professional presence and reflect on best practices.

Scenario 2 (Alternative Flow)

  • Neutral introduces the meeting and each character enters or joins in turn.
  • Scenes highlight nervous language, arrogance, disorganization, and strong professional presence.
  • Monologues give insight into each character’s mindset and behavior.
  • The scenario emphasizes clothing, engagement, body language, and verbal communication.

Key Stats and Takeaways

Key perception factors: Visual appearance, tone of voice, and word choice strongly influence how others perceive you, as discussed in research on communication and professional presence.

First impressions form quickly, so professional presence in the first few seconds of an interaction is especially important.

Use the skit and the outline to prompt discussion, ask for audience feedback, and reinforce key points about online presence, phone etiquette, body language, clothing, hygiene, and communication.

Data Science Project
Identifying Actionable Treatment Variation in Episodes of Low Back Pain

Source code from a project with Blue Cross Blue Shield of Massachusetts, with data and additional files omitted to respect intellectual property and confidentiality.

Model artifact: Link to the fitted model HTML

Background

Low back pain affects a large majority of adults at some point, and treatment expenditures in the United States are substantial each year.

Despite extensive clinical guidance, practice patterns still vary widely, so this project focused on identifying where providers can adjust care pathways to improve outcomes while reducing overall medical costs.

Data Overview

The analysis used roughly 33,000 treatment episodes of low back pain from 2015–2016, focusing on how costs concentrate in a small subset of cases.

The highest-cost 5 percent of episodes accounted for more than half of total spending, and all of these high-cost cases included surgery at some point, within a clinically homogeneous group of patients with similar health status.

Objective

The central question was why patients with similar low back pain conditions can end up on very different treatment trajectories, with some receiving expensive surgical procedures while others receive non-surgical orthopedic care at a much lower cost.

The model supported an analysis of which factors are associated with these divergent pathways, helping highlight levers that clinicians could adjust to steer patients toward effective, lower-cost options when appropriate.

Methods

  1. Isolate patients into an approximately clinically homogeneous cohort to reduce confounding by baseline health differences.
  2. Identify potentially important and actionable features with clinical input, such as imaging use, physical therapy utilization, and key medical history characteristics.
  3. Address class imbalance so that the model can learn from both common and rare outcome patterns.
  4. Fit a random forest to a bootstrapped training sample and evaluate performance on a validation subset.
  5. Interpret feature importance in close collaboration with clinicians to translate model signals into meaningful, actionable insights.

  • Cost concentration analysis
